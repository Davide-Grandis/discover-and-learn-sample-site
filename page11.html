Space Exploration

From Sputnik to Mars rovers — humanity's quest to reach the stars.

The Space Age Begins

On October 4, 1957, the Soviet Union launched Sputnik 1 — a polished metal sphere barely larger than a beach ball — and changed the course of history. Its simple radio beep, audible to amateur radio operators worldwide, announced that humanity had entered the Space Age.

Just twelve years later, on July 20, 1969, Neil Armstrong became the first person to set foot on the Moon, watched live by an estimated 600 million people.

The New Space Race

The 21st century has seen a dramatic shift in space exploration. Private companies now play a central role:

  • SpaceX — reusable rockets, Starlink constellation, and ambitions for Mars colonisation
  • Blue Origin — suborbital tourism and heavy-lift launch vehicles
  • Rocket Lab — small satellite launches with the Electron rocket
  • ISRO (India) — cost-effective missions including Chandrayaan lunar probes

Meanwhile, NASA's Artemis programme aims to return humans to the Moon and establish a permanent lunar presence as a stepping stone to Mars.

"That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind." — Neil Armstrong

What's Next?

The coming decades promise extraordinary developments:

  1. Permanent human presence on the Moon by the late 2020s
  2. First crewed mission to Mars, potentially in the 2030s
  3. Expansion of space tourism beyond Earth orbit
  4. Search for biosignatures on Europa and Enceladus
  5. Interstellar probes using light-sail technology
