The Solar System

Journey through our cosmic neighbourhood — planets, moons, and everything in between.

Our Place in Space

The Solar System formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a giant molecular cloud. At its centre sits the Sun, a G-type main-sequence star that contains 99.86% of the system's total mass.

Eight planets orbit the Sun, divided into two groups: the rocky inner planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars) and the gas and ice giants of the outer system (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune).

Beyond the Planets

The Solar System doesn't end at Neptune. Beyond it lies:

  • The Kuiper Belt — a ring of icy bodies including Pluto and Eris
  • The Scattered Disc — a sparsely populated region of icy minor planets
  • The Oort Cloud — a theoretical shell of trillions of comets extending nearly a light-year from the Sun
