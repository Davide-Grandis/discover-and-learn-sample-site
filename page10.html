Photography Basics

Master composition, lighting, and exposure to capture stunning images.

The Exposure Triangle

Every photograph is the result of three settings working together — aperture, shutter speed, and ISO. Understanding how they interact is the foundation of photography.

SettingWhat It ControlsCreative Effect
Aperture (f-stop)How much light enters the lensDepth of field (blurry vs. sharp background)
Shutter SpeedHow long the sensor is exposedMotion blur vs. freeze action
ISOSensor sensitivity to lightBrightness vs. noise/grain

A wide aperture (f/1.8) creates a shallow depth of field — perfect for portraits. A small aperture (f/16) keeps everything sharp — ideal for landscapes.

Composition Rules

Great composition guides the viewer's eye and creates visual interest. Here are the most useful techniques:

  • Rule of Thirds — place your subject at the intersection of imaginary grid lines that divide the frame into nine equal parts
  • Leading Lines — use roads, fences, or rivers to draw the eye toward the subject
  • Framing — use doorways, windows, or branches to create a natural frame
  • Symmetry — reflections and balanced compositions create a sense of calm
  • Negative Space — leaving empty space around the subject emphasises it

Light Is Everything

Photography literally means "drawing with light." The quality, direction, and colour of light fundamentally shape your image.

  • Golden Hour — the hour after sunrise and before sunset produces warm, soft light
  • Blue Hour — just before sunrise or after sunset, the sky turns a deep blue
  • Overcast days — clouds act as a giant diffuser, creating even, flattering light
  • Harsh midday sun — creates strong shadows; use it creatively or seek shade
"Your first 10,000 photographs are your worst." — Henri Cartier-Bresson

Getting Started

  1. Shoot in manual mode to learn how aperture, shutter speed, and ISO interact
  2. Practise with subjects you love — landscapes, people, food, architecture
  3. Review your photos critically and identify what works and what doesn't
  4. Study the work of photographers you admire
  5. Remember: the best camera is the one you have with you
