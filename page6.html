Mountains of the World

Peaks that touch the sky — geology, climbing history, and breathtaking views.

How Mountains Form

Mountains are created by tectonic forces that push, fold, and fracture Earth's crust over millions of years. The Himalayas, for example, are still rising at roughly 5 mm per year as the Indian plate collides with the Eurasian plate.

Other mountains are volcanic in origin — Mount Kilimanjaro and Mount Fuji were both built by eruptions over hundreds of thousands of years.

Mountain Ecosystems

As altitude increases, distinct ecological zones emerge:

  • Montane forests — dense woodland at the base, home to deer, bears, and countless birds
  • Subalpine zone — shorter trees, meadows of wildflowers in summer
  • Alpine zone — above the treeline; grasses, mosses, and hardy shrubs
  • Nival zone — permanent snow and ice; only extremophile organisms survive
